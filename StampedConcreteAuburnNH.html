An Auburn homeowner near Pingree Hill wanted a new patio and pool deck, but their property falls within the Lake Massabesic protected watershed — Manchester's primary drinking water supply. Two contractors had turned down the project due to the environmental compliance requirements involved.

The project was approved on first review, and the homeowner now has the outdoor living space they dreamed of — fully compliant and built to last decades.

Auburn Stamped Concrete FAQ

Common questions we hear from Auburn homeowners.

Are there special rules for concrete work near Lake Massabesic?

Yes. Properties within the Lake Massabesic watershed (managed by Manchester Water Works) have specific setback and stormwater management requirements. We're fully experienced with these regulations and handle all compliance during planning at no additional cost to you.

Will stamped concrete work on Auburn's rocky terrain?

Absolutely. Auburn has a mix of bedrock ledge and glacial deposits. For rocky sites, we adjust our excavation approach and base preparation to work with the terrain rather than against it. Our 40+ years of experience with NH geology means we've seen and solved every condition.

Do I need a permit for a patio in Auburn?

Auburn typically does not require a building permit for standard ground-level patios. However, watershed zone properties and lots near wetlands may need additional approvals from the Conservation Commission. We verify all requirements during our free site assessment.

What's the best season to pour concrete in Auburn?

Our season runs late April through mid-October. Auburn's well-drained soils often allow us to start a bit earlier in spring than lower-lying communities. Fall installations benefit from cooler temperatures and ideal curing conditions for maximum durability.

How do you handle Auburn properties with ledge?

Rocky ledge is common in Auburn, especially along Bunker Hill Road and Pingree Hill. When we encounter ledge, we adapt our base preparation — sometimes using a thicker gravel overlay instead of excavating into rock. The result is equally durable and often more cost-effective.
